Later that year, a Free Officers Movement led by Abdulkarim Qasim overthrew Iraq's imperialist-aligned Hashemite monarchy, a revolution that also brought the Iraqi Communist Party to the heights of power, a power they also used to violently overreach against their rivals, namely the Ba'ath.


These revolutionary projects did not last long. In 1961, a military coup pulled Syria out of the United Arab Republic. In February 1963, the Ba'ath and other nationalists overthrew Qasem's government in Iraq, and the Ba'athists unleashed a campaign of bloody terror against the Communists.


In March 1963, Syrian Ba'athists seized power in a coup. In November 1963, Iraqi nationalists ousted their one-time Ba'athist allies from power. In 1966, the Syrian Ba'ath Military Committee, led by figures like Salah Jadid and Hafez al-Assad, launched a coup that sidelined Ba'ath traditional leadership, people like Michelle Aflaq and Saladin al-Battar.


This history we've been telling is rife with vehement and often violent splits of all sorts between Syrian and Iraqi Ba'athists, and among Syrian and Iraqi Ba'athists. And there will be more such conflicts to come.
