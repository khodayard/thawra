Thawra Ep. 9 – Palestine on the Road to Revolution

Last Episode Recap:

Our last installment covered the first decade of the movement of Arab Nationalists, founded as the Arab Nationalist Youth in 1951. The movement was significantly led by Palestinians and built a substantial presence in Beirut and Jordan, becoming a truly powerful force in Kuwait.

This organization ultimately became critical to the history of Arab anti-colonial radicalism in general and to the Palestinian national liberation movement in particular. It led to the creation of multiple radical Marxist groups, including the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine and the Democratic Front for the Liberation of Palestine, the major left-wing factions of the struggle. It also played a key role in the founding of South Yemen’s National Liberation Front, which, after the British departure in 1967, established the socialist People’s Democratic Republic of Yemen, and the Dhofar Liberation Front, which carried out an armed struggle against the Sultan of Muscat in Oman from the mid-1960s to the mid-1970s. These important movements will be discussed in upcoming episodes.



This Episode Overview:

This episode covers Palestinian national liberation politics throughout the 1950s, as they were pieced together among a people expelled from their land and dispersed across the region by the Nakba. Organizations, alliances, and theories of change emerged in universities, cities, and refugee camps surrounding Palestine.

We culminate our discussion with the foundation of Fatah in 1959, marking a break from the pan-Arab mold that had previously shaped mass-oriented nationalist politics in Palestine. Fatah was the first organization for Palestinians led by Palestinians, focused primarily on Palestinian liberation. This episode tells the story of the beginning of the Palestinian national liberation movement as we know it today.



Interview:

DANIEL DENVIR: Okay, here’s Abdel Razzaq Takriti, who teaches history at Rice University. A scholar of Arab and Palestinian revolutionary movements, he is the author of Monsoon Revolution: Republicans, Sultans, and Empires in Oman and the co-author of the Palestinian Revolution digital humanities website—an incredible resource that will be back online soon.

I want to zoom out from the movement of Arab Nationalists and talk about Palestinian politics more broadly during the 1950s. In the years immediately following the Nakba, the locus of Palestinian resistance was not in the occupied territories—because those territories were not yet occupied. They only became occupied in 1967 when Israel conquered Gaza from Egypt and the West Bank, including East Jerusalem, from Jordan.

So in the 1950s, we had large numbers of Palestinians, including many refugees, living throughout Jordan, Syria, Lebanon, Egypt, and Kuwait, alongside Palestinians who remained within the new state of Israel under military rule at the time. What did Palestinian politics look like in the 1950s? With Palestine’s mandate-era leadership defeated, the Palestinian people dispersed and fragmented, and Palestinian politics often expressed through broader pan-Arab nationalist movements, what was Palestinian politics in this period?

ABDEL RAZZAQ TAKRITI: When you look at the 1940s and late ’40s, there was a collapse in 1948 of the old party structure in Palestine. There had been political parties specific to Palestine, but with the loss of 78% of Palestinian land—absorbed by the settler-colonial Zionist movement and renamed as the state of Israel—the entire old party structure was destroyed.

These parties were not primarily ideological; they were centered around alliances and prominent figures. The primary debate among them concerned the extent of cooperation or confrontation with British authorities. One wing, led by the Mufti Haj Amin al-Husseini, was less inclined toward strong relations with the British and took a more combative stance, though still within the bounds of the British Mandate. Another wing, led by Raghib al-Nashashibi, was seen as closer to the British and later to figures like Prince Abdullah of the Hashemite dynasty.

With the collapse of Palestine and the occupation of the majority of it, the term “occupied territories” was used after 1948 by Palestinians and Arabs to refer to what is now the Israeli state. Today, many listeners may associate “occupied territories” with the lands occupied in 1967, but originally, it referred to the entirety of what became Israel.

For the indigenous Palestinian and surrounding Arab populations, the legitimacy of a settler-colonial state was never accepted.

DENVIR: Yes. It’s not just settlers in the West Bank today, but the entirety of historic Palestine was settled and colonized.

TAKRITI: Exactly. That’s the factual reality. It’s just framed differently by different audiences to make it more acceptable in certain narratives. The people who were expelled from their villages, cities, and towns were not the ones framing this discourse. They were the ones who had bullets and bombs dropped on them and were forced to give up their land to incoming settlers—some of whom had barely been in Palestine for two, three, or four years.

This is a difficult subject to discuss in many Western countries, partly because of the horrors of the Holocaust. But from an Arab or Palestinian perspective, these horrors were inflicted by Europe upon its own Jewish citizens for no fault other than their identity. Genocidal European ideologies and practices were not Palestinian or Arab ideologies; they were European crimes against Europeans.

DENVIR: And as we’ll discuss in future episodes, 1967 not only marked a new historic phase in the Palestinian national liberation struggle, but Israel’s occupation of the West Bank, Gaza, and East Jerusalem also retroactively normalized the 78% of historic Palestine conquered by Zionists in 1948.

TAKRITI: In some circles, yes. And that normalization became a major subject of debate within the Palestinian revolutionary movement. But at the time, nobody accepted the idea that Palestine was lost and that this should be considered a settled fact.

As I mentioned earlier regarding the movement of Arab Nationalists, part of their focus was on defeating the notion that the loss of 78% of Palestinian territory was permanent. They were determined to counteract any normalization of this reality, particularly since only a minority of the indigenous Palestinian population remained in those lands.

At the same time, it is important to note that Zionist propaganda has often misrepresented the Palestinian demand for return as a call for mass expulsion or violence against Jewish settlers. We must be clear: the majority of Palestinians were not focused on expelling settlers but on securing their right to return. In Arabic, this is known as Haq Al-Awda (The Right of Return). They were not advocating for expulsion but for their own return to their land.

Of course, many of the settlers had no prior ties to Palestine; they arrived from Poland, Ukraine, and other parts of the world. Later, large numbers of Arab Jews were also absorbed into the Zionist project. We’ll discuss that in more depth when we talk about Iraq and related topics in a future episode.



DENVIR: It says a lot about the Zionist and colonialist mindset that the idea of the right to return, or Palestine being free from the river to the sea, immediately provokes the specter of violence against the colonizer. That’s the first thing that comes to mind.

TAKRITI: Which is pretty classic.

In South Africa, for example, the argument was constantly made: How can we put the white population under the mercy—and I put that in quotes—of the black population? But the reality was that the black population was the original people of the land, the majority, and there was nothing inherently wrong with people living together. The fact that the white population would no longer be the ruling majority did not justify excluding the black population from political participation, nor from their natural national and civil rights.

These same questions applied in Palestine, in South Africa, and in Algeria. The French, for instance, insisted that the Pieds-Noirs should never be subjected to Algerian rule. Ultimately, the Pieds-Noirs left, not because they were forcibly expelled, but because they feared Algerians. The official Algerian ideology was not to expel French settlers, but many left because they had internalized the myth of the monstrous native—this long-standing colonial fear that the oppressed would seek vengeance. This myth has a deep history.

Even in Haiti, for example, Toussaint Louverture was remarkably open to white slave owners. He was willing to tolerate them, despite their history, until they gave him no choice. The French waged a genocidal campaign, killed him, and ultimately forced a more radical response. That’s when Jean-Jacques Dessalines removed white from the Haitian flag. Yet even then, he still included certain white allies under the category of black—meaning, people who fought against slavery, regardless of skin color.

I don’t want to go too tangential here, but these colonial patterns are a global phenomenon.

DENVIR: That’s important because the common juxtaposition of the Algerian versus South African models, which is frequently applied to Palestine, is itself based on a misreading of Algeria’s national liberation project.

TAKRITI: Absolutely.

People often claim that the Pieds-Noirs were expelled, but I challenge anyone to find evidence that this was the FLN’s official position. It wasn’t part of their program. The Pieds-Noirs left because they were terrified of their own myths. And despite that, about 80,000 French citizens remained in Algeria after independence.

Now, I’m not saying that anti-colonial movements have never engaged in violence against settlers. The Mau Mau in Kenya, the FLN in Algeria, the Palestinian liberation movement, and the South African liberation struggle all saw cases where settlers were killed. But the idea that the native population is inherently genocidal, that they are the eradicationist force, is completely ridiculous.

DENVIR: It’s pure projection. The colonizer is the eradicationist force, while the native population has consistently shown far more willingness to coexist in peace and equality than the colonizers have.

TAKRITI: All the time.

Even within the United States, I often reflect on this. White slave owners were obsessed with the fear of enslaved people’s rebellions, and even today, so-called race riots are framed as a terrifying threat. The narrative goes: Black youth are going to overrun neighborhoods—as if the actual victims of racial oppression are the aggressors. It’s outrageous. It’s self-projection.

It’s deeply painful to witness because it’s so unjust, yet this is how colonial systems function. They rely on ideological fantasies to justify themselves. Colonizers must perpetuate these myths to rationalize their actions. Otherwise, how could they sleep at night? They invent these stories to reconcile their self-image as liberal subjects while committing horrific crimes. Scholars have documented this for a long time.

DENVIR: What did Palestinian politics look like in the absence of an organized, politically independent national liberation movement? Maybe we can start on a sociological level—what were Palestinian everyday realities like in the decade after the Nakba, and how did those conditions shape political developments?

TAKRITI: Initially, the struggle was simply to return. These were refugee communities, expelled from their land, living in exile. Some were internal refugees—Palestinians who had fled or were forced out of areas occupied in 1948 and ended up in the West Bank or Gaza. Others remained inside the new state of Israel but were prevented from returning to their homes, becoming internally displaced people. To this day, many Palestinian citizens of Israel are still barred from reclaiming their lands.

Beyond that, large numbers of refugees ended up in Lebanon, Syria, Jordan, Egypt, and Iraq, depending largely on geography. People from northern Galilee, particularly the western side, fled to Lebanon. Those from the eastern side, around Safad, mostly ended up in Syria. That’s why major refugee camps like Yarmouk housed so many families from Safad. Palestinians from the Jordan Valley often settled in Jordan, while those from the Haifa district either ended up in Lebanon or the northwestern West Bank.

Southern Palestinians—especially those from the Negev, Beersheba, and areas south of Jaffa like Isdud and Majdal Askelon (which were renamed Ashdod and Ashkelon)—were mostly forced into Gaza. This was part of Operation Yoav, the brutal ethnic cleansing campaign after which Israel’s current defense minister, Yoav Gallant, was named. His father was an officer in that operation, and now Gallant himself is carrying out another campaign of ethnic cleansing in Gaza today, with openly genocidal intent.

Palestinian politics was shaped by the presence of these massive refugee communities. Living in refugee camps transformed political consciousness. Camps brought together people from different villages and towns, fostering broader discussions beyond the local level. The very existence of the camps reshaped Palestinian political organization and identity, laying the foundation for future movements.



DENVIR: This situation is similar to industrialization or urbanization processes, where people transition from rural or village life into denser urban networks.

TAKRITI: Absolutely. It also alters the class structure. Initially, the society was predominantly made up of landowning people who worked the land. Now, those people are coming together in camps. Many have lost their land, and some are working on land owned by others. Refugees are joining agricultural communities, and in some cases, refugee camps even emerge in rural areas. Some refugees settle in areas like the Jordan Valley and parts of Jericho, where there are agricultural projects. There’s also a process of proletarianization happening, as the camps provide a working-class resource for people. In places like Lebanon, where people are prevented from working, there’s an additional form of proletarianization. Many refugees face significant struggles to make a living.

One key thing to note is the class composition of most refugee camps. The wealthier individuals, for the most part, didn’t end up in the camps, and neither did the urban middle or upper classes. This challenges the myth that all Palestinian refugees live in camps. People often misunderstand the term “refugee.” For example, I am a Palestinian refugee, but I’m a university professor at an American institution, living in Houston, Texas. It’s not accurate to assume that every Palestinian refugee must live in a camp in the region. Refugees are defined by their loss of country, their inability to return, and the inheritance of that status by their descendants.

There’s a larger effort, particularly by Israelis, Americans, and Western powers, to create exceptions around the Palestinian situation. This has been happening since the beginning, trying to undermine the refugee status. They argue that the category of refugees should not pass down to the next generation, but I point out that this isn’t the case with other global refugee populations.

DENVIR: The Intercept just revealed a New York Times internal memo, a style guide, that cautions reporters against calling refugee camps in Gaza as such.

TAKRITI: Exactly. This is part of the effort to diminish the category of Palestinian refugees and downplay their numbers. If you claim that Palestinian refugees no longer exist, you erase their right to return, and that essentially gives the settlers the victory. It creates the narrative that there is no indigenous population. This is a way of eradicating the future of indigenous Palestinians.

From the moment of the Nakba, Palestinians resisted the humanitarian framing of their situation. They consistently pushed for repatriation, not resettlement, and this resistance was strong. Even when they feared that Arab states might push for resettlement, some refugee camps opposed any infrastructure that would suggest permanence, such as roads or services. At first, there was intense resistance to anything that could imply that people would stay in these camps. It’s a natural human instinct to want to return home, and refugees didn’t want to live in these camps.

At the start, the conditions in these camps were horrendous. When the Nakba occurred, it was a harsh winter, and people lived in makeshift tents. Over time, they started using zinc and metal structures to build shelters, but the conditions remained difficult for several years. Eventually, more permanent structures began to emerge, although in Lebanon, there were laws that restricted construction within the camps. The environment was extremely repressive, but despite this, refugees continued to organize within the camps. The state was worried about this organizing, but it still happened.

In Lebanon, the social mobilization within the camps was a blend of traditional forms of community organization and more modern ideological party structures. The older generation still relied on kinship and geographic ties, and this is visible even today in the way Palestinians organize in camps. People from the same village tend to group together in specific parts of the camp, recreating the kinship networks and geographic connections that existed before the displacement. The camp becomes a physical representation of all the villages in one space, but the human connections still reflect the past forms of social organization.

This organization also meant that some leadership structures, especially those based on village elders or prominent figures, continued to hold influence. In Bedouin communities that became refugees, traditional leaders, like the Bedouin sheikhs, retained their authority. These leaders maintained their prestige and held meetings where people would gather to discuss the issues of the day, including political matters. This tradition of community gathering, known as the “Modafa,” was crucial for maintaining a sense of belonging and solidarity, and it continued within the camps, where people would discuss return, conspiracies, and the Palestinian cause.

DENVIR: Did the everyday politics of the camps create an opening for modern ideological parties like the Ba’ath, the Communists, or the Arab nationalist movements that we’ve been discussing in this series?

TAKRITI: Yes, modern political parties were able to enter the camps in unique ways through these existing structures. For example, one of the members of the Arab nationalist movement in Lebanon, Salah Salah, whom I interviewed, joined the movement by distributing the party’s newspaper within these circles. Many people in the older population couldn’t read, but since he was a young person who had been educated, he would read the newspaper aloud to the village elders. They were curious about the content, especially about issues like conspiracies to resettle refugees rather than repatriate them. This kind of material resonated with them. Over time, when there was a call to demonstrate, young people from these camps would approach the elders, asking for their support in organizing protests—against resettlement plans, the Baghdad Pact, and what they saw as imperialist conspiracies aimed at Palestine and the region. The elders would lend their authority, and people would rally behind them. So, there was a fascinating intersection between the old social networks and the new ideological movements.

Over time, party structures became more prominent, and the modern political parties gradually overtook some of the traditional kinship-based organizing. However, the old networks never disappeared entirely. It’s interesting to note that even with the urbanization process in the camps, a part of the village mentality remained. The camp still contained elements of old Palestine, and this dynamic persisted in how people affiliated and congregated. However, I don’t want to romanticize this by any means, as it also led to some conservatism in social norms, among other complications. Many of the radical movements wanted to utilize these traditional structures, but at the same time, they were also trying to eradicate them. It created a complex dynamic within the camps.

Beyond the sociological aspect, there was another form of organizing taking place, particularly in student circles, which were crucial for the creation of a politically independent Palestinian liberation struggle. These student movements were foundational for rebuilding the Palestinian body politic in this period. It’s helpful to think of this in generational terms. Some individuals had experience in Palestinian politics before the Nakba and were still young enough to continue their political work after it. These people, like Abdullah Rimawi, who founded the Ba’ath Party, were not tainted by the Nakba in the same way as those who belonged to the old, official parties, which were seen as having lost Palestine. Figures from the Communist Party also continued their work, but they had a different trajectory.

Then, a new generation emerged, one that was truly shaped by the Nakba. These were the Nakba generation—those who had been displaced but were old enough to remember Palestine. Shafiq al-Hout, a former PLO representative in Lebanon, who joined the Communist movement in the 1950s and became very active in Palestinian work, described this generation as people who “knew the smells, sights, and sounds of Palestine” but were expelled from it. They were young enough to be deeply involved in organizing for the return of Palestinians. Typically, they were teenagers or young adults—around 14 to 18 years old.

Many of these individuals ended up in universities and high schools, particularly those who had performed well academically or came from lower-middle-class backgrounds. This represented a new class dynamic, with the new leadership largely emerging from the lower-middle class rather than from the traditional Palestinian elite, the wealthy landowners or notable families. These students were often the sons and daughters of small traders or local figures. The key to their political socialization was the high school or university environment. It was at these institutions that many of them found their political voice and became active in the Palestinian cause.

DENVIR: How did these new Palestinian student movements and environments serve as the incubator for a politically independent Palestinian liberation struggle, culminating in the foundation of Fatah?

Arafat belonged to a conservative, yet modern political framework. While his approach was rooted in activating various social networks and kinship structures, he also understood how to navigate the modern political landscape effectively. He had a knack for dealing with authorities and knew how to work behind the scenes to organize successfully. Arafat’s political base was distinct from those of his competitors in student elections, like the Arab nationalists, the Ba’athists, and the Communists. He resisted attempts to absorb Palestinian politics into broader ideologies or movements. Although not a formal member, Arafat struck deals with the Muslim Brotherhood, as he was studying engineering at King Fuad University (later Cairo University). His background allowed him to form strategic alliances with different political forces.

Arafat’s unique base came from his ties to Gaza, which at the time was the only part of Palestine where people could publicly identify as Palestinian. Most of Palestine had fallen under Israeli control, and those who remained under Israeli rule could no longer express their Palestinian identity openly.

DENVIR: And much of the rest was annexed by Jordan.

TAKRITI: Exactly.

DENVIR: And then Gaza was administered by Egypt, but not annexed.

TAKRITI: Exactly. In Gaza, you could still openly identify as Palestinian. The Egyptians allowed the formation of what was called the All-Palestine Government, which was declared in Gaza with its supposed capital in Jerusalem. This government briefly established itself during the 1948 war, but it was ultimately short-lived. King Abdullah of Jordan made sure it was politically undermined at an Arab level, preventing it from gaining real recognition. That’s one of the reasons Palestine never appeared on the map as a state—something that deeply frustrated figures like Yasser Arafat and others. This frustration became a defining theme within what people sometimes call Palestinian nationalism.

Personally, I don’t favor that term. I think it’s more accurate to call it Palestinian patriotism, because there was never really a movement seeking to completely detach Palestine from the broader Arab world. Palestinians were always deeply integrated into the region, arguably more so than most other Arab communities. This includes even the group that coalesced around Yasser Arafat, which later became Fatah.

Now, this group first emerged in Cairo. It was primarily composed of students from Gaza, but it also included Palestinians from other areas who had ended up in Cairo for their education. Over time, as more scholarships and free education opportunities were offered under Nasser, students from Jordan and elsewhere also began studying in Egypt. This was part of a broader trend of progressive economic reforms in the Arab world, particularly under Nasser, which had a significant impact on working-class and refugee communities. While Nasser had clear ideological limits—he wasn’t pushing for communism—his policies nonetheless provided educational opportunities for many Palestinians. Similar opportunities also came from Iraq and other parts of the Arab world, but we can discuss that later.

What’s key here is the emergence of this Palestinian student body in Cairo. These students arrived with various political orientations. Some, like Salah Khalaf—who later became known as Abu Iyad—had a background in the Muslim Brotherhood. He later leaned more toward the left and played a major role in Fatah, but his early experience was as an organizer for the Brotherhood. Others, like Abu al-Adib, had similar backgrounds. Arafat relied on figures like them to mobilize in Cairo, and he also built strong connections with Muslim Brotherhood youth leaders in Gaza. Some of these contacts weren’t his classmates in Egypt but were crucial organizers back home.

One of these key figures was Khalil al-Wazir, better known as Abu Jihad, who eventually became Arafat’s second-in-command in Fatah. Together, these figures built personal networks that would later become the foundation for political mobilization.

Years later, in 1967, she reconnected with Abu Iyad, who asked her, “Do you remember me, May?” At first, she didn’t recognize him—he had aged and lost some hair—but once she realized who he was, their old connection allowed her to take on a leadership role in Fatah.

This is just one example of how these personal networks shaped the early years of Fatah. What’s interesting is that most of its founding members came from either Muslim Brotherhood or Ba’athist backgrounds, with some coming from splintered Communist circles. Very few came from the Arab Nationalist Movement, which took a different trajectory and, by the 1960s, became a competing political structure. That division would later define different factions within the Palestinian movement, but we can explore that further in the next discussion.

DENVIR: What’s happening elsewhere across the region among Palestinian refugees? And how do these geographically dispersed circles begin to intersect?

TAKRITI: We’ve already covered the Gaza network, which was centered around Cairo. But there was another important network emerging in Damascus. Syria provided a more open environment for Palestinian refugees, allowing for political organizing in ways that other Arab states didn’t. At times, Syria even had periods of democratic freedoms where Palestinians could engage in political life. Many Palestinian refugees in Syria became active in pan-Arab movements and, in some cases, in internationalist movements like the Communist Party.

At the same time, another wing of Palestinian activists in Syria entered pan-Islamic politics, either through the Muslim Brotherhood or Hizb ut-Tahrir. Some of these early organizers later became key figures in Fatah. One example is Khaled al-Hassan, who went on to head Fatah’s foreign relations department for many years, along with his brother Hani al-Hassan. Both came from a background of Islamic organizing in Syrian refugee camps.

But there was also a more secular movement developing among Palestinian students, particularly in high schools. These students felt the need for a distinctly Palestinian political space and founded a group called Arab Falastin (“The Arabs of Palestine”). While it wasn’t a large organization—only a few dozen members—it played an influential role in shaping future leaders of Fatah. Some members later joined the Ba’ath Party, others joined different factions, but the experience of organizing in a specifically Palestinian framework stayed with them. One key figure from this group was Zaeim Abd al-Hamid, among others.

The real turning point came when the Cairo and Damascus networks began merging, and this happened in Kuwait. After losing everything in 1948, many Palestinians sought education as their way forward, with the best job prospects being in Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, or other Gulf states. Palestinians from Cairo and Damascus backgrounds found themselves working in Kuwait and the Gulf, where their networks converged. Some settled in Saudi Arabia’s Eastern Province, particularly in Aramco, where a number of prominent Fatah members emerged, including early figures like Jamal Adwan. Others established themselves in Qatar, where Mahmoud Abbas (Abu Mazen)—who, in my view, is a disgrace to any revolutionary tradition—became involved. He came from the Damascus experience, as his family had been expelled from Safad to Syria.

At the heart of this network-building were two key figures: Yasser Arafat and Adil Abd al-Karim. Abd al-Karim, who is now largely erased from history, was instrumental in bringing the Cairo and Damascus factions together. He later became a professor of mathematics and left the movement early due to a dispute with Arafat. But at the time, he and Arafat were the main forces uniting these different groups. Abd al-Karim had a Damascus-based network, while Arafat led the Cairo group.

I emphasize this point because some conspiracy theories suggest that Arafat had little to do with Fatah’s founding—this is simply not true. I’ve conducted extensive primary research, including interviews with hundreds of people and reviews of countless documents. There is no doubt that Arafat played a central role.

In those early days, four figures were considered the core leadership of Fatah:

1.        Yasser Arafat (from the Cairo network)

2.        Adil Abd al-Karim (from the Damascus network)

3.        Khalil al-Wazir (Abu Jihad) (a key organizer and strategist)

4.        Abdullah al-Dannan (from the Damascus side, who later became a renowned linguist)

Within Fatah circles, they were often called The Big Four.

DENVIR: What was their vision for Palestinian liberation? What revolutionary theory did they propose for decolonizing Palestine?

TAKRITI: Their strategy was heavily influenced by the Algerian Revolution. At the time, Arafat was working as an engineer in Kuwait, and Abd al-Karim was a teacher. They looked to Algeria’s FLN as a model for how an indigenous liberation movement could succeed.

This vision set Fatah apart from the Movement of Arab Nationalists (MAN), which had a different approach. The MAN believed that Palestine could only be liberated if the Arab world first underwent a broader revolutionary transformation. Their view was that the Arab states needed to unite and build military strength before they could take on the Zionist project, which was backed by the West. According to them, the loss of Palestine was due to weak Arab armies, fragmented Arab states, and a lack of social cohesion. Their goal was to overhaul the entire regional system, strengthen Arab armies, and then use this military power to liberate Palestine. This was a more traditional Arab nationalist perspective, similar to that of the Ba’athists at the time.

Fatah, however, took a different path. Arafat, Abd al-Karim, and their comrades believed that Palestinians themselves had to lead the fight for liberation. Their argument was that in 1948, Arab armies had disarmed Palestinian fighters, leaving them vulnerable to Zionist militias. Instead of supporting local resistance, these armies pursued the political interests of the states they represented. For Fatah, this was a fundamental betrayal.

Their conclusion was that Palestinians could not rely on Arab armies to liberate Palestine. Instead, they needed to organize their own armed resistance, just as the FLN had done in Algeria. They saw the role of Arab states not as direct military actors, but as logistical supporters—providing weapons, funding, and safe havens for the Palestinian armed struggle. Just as Tunisia and Morocco had opened their borders to the FLN, Fatah envisioned Arab states arming and backing Palestinian fighters, who would carry out the liberation of Palestine themselves.

This became the core of Fatah’s revolutionary doctrine: armed struggle led by Palestinians, with support from Arab states but not dependency on their militaries. This was a radical departure from the traditional Arab nationalist approach and set Fatah on a distinct path in the Palestinian liberation movement.

DENVIR: Earlier in the interview, you mentioned that refugees, in the years after the Nakba, attempted to return to the homes from which they had been expelled within what was now the state of Israel. What did that look like, and how did the Israeli state respond?

TAKRITI: Generally, there were numerous attempts by refugees to cross the border—some succeeded, but many failed. In the early 1950s, hope for success still lingered. There were individual cases, especially immediately after the 1948 war. For example:

•        The family of Palestine’s most renowned author and poet, Mahmoud Darwish, went back to their village after being expelled and crossing the border.

These cases were exceptional. For the vast majority of refugees, repatriation was not possible, even as they persistently demanded it. Today, their voices continue to be raised, and there are ongoing efforts to cancel the legal category of Palestinian refugees. For instance, figures like Jared Kushner, in coordination with Netanyahu, are attempting to eliminate this category. In Gaza, it is argued that these individuals are descendants of refugees; under international law, the descendants of those expelled remain refugees until a political settlement allows them to return home.

In the 1950s, refugees engaged in extensive discussions about the next steps, which led to the formation of various organizations. Some of these formations had a strictly Palestinian emphasis, as seen in the following:

•        Youth groups and circles: Prominent in Damascus, Cairo, and especially among student movements.

•        Movements with different orientations: For example, the Muslim Brotherhood trend was particularly strong in Gaza due to its proximity to Egypt (where the Muslim Brotherhood originated) and on the Jordanian side, where it was supported by the state. Many were also impressed that Egyptian Muslim Brotherhood volunteers had fought alongside Palestinians during the 1948 war.

However, the Brotherhood faced internal challenges with its youth wing. The student side was led by a young man named Khalil al-Wazir—later known as Abu Jihad—who eventually became the number two in Fatah under Yasser Arafat. Abu Jihad and other youths joined the Brotherhood in the early 1950s with the belief that it could help liberate Palestine. They soon discovered that the Brotherhood leadership advocated a gradual approach. Their strategy emphasized:

•        Mobilizing for Da’awa (the spreading of the Islamic word)

•        Building self-reliance and organizing society

•        Delaying immediate military mobilization against dispossession and land usurpation

DENVIR: It’s a sort of religious, conservative outlook that says we need to turn towards God and become a holy people in order to merit winning Palestine back—a turning away from the forces that allowed for its loss in the first place.

TAKRITI: That is a significant part of it. Another crucial element was the need for organizational infrastructure and deep social work to build a solid base. To be fair to the Brotherhood, they excelled in this area. Their politics were closely connected to social work, as evidenced by their efforts in establishing:

•        Charities

•        Medical youth support centers

•        Various educational initiatives

This work helped them build a strong presence in society, particularly around the institution of the mosque.

DENVIR: In previous episodes, we discussed how they were the masters of the war of position.

TAKRITI: Very true. However, this approach did not serve them well in the Palestinian arena during the early 1950s. At that time, there was a strong sentiment demanding the liberation of the land. For instance, if you were a youth in Gaza in 1954—imagine being in Abu Jihad’s shoes—you would have been acutely aware of the constant Israeli attacks on Gaza. These attacks were justified as measures to protect the state of Israel from “infiltrators,” a term used for refugees who had been expelled and were attempting to return or engage in minor cross-border operations to avenge their losses.

Abu Jihad and his colleagues began organizing small-scale cross-border sabotage operations. They targeted nearby kibbutzim—institutions that had taken lands from Palestinians—by sabotaging water tanks and disrupting network operations. The primary goal of these operations was to disturb the normal life of the settler colonialists and delay further colonization of the area.

TAKRITI: We see the beginnings of armed struggle emerging in different locations. Initiatives arose from Gaza and were carried out by various individuals, including Abu Jihad—who later became the number two in Fatah. He had organized the youth wing of the Muslim Brotherhood, and they began attacking specific installations and carrying out sabotage operations across the border. This occurred while Israel was engaging in heavy-handed tactics in Gaza. The Israeli outlook was akin to that of a bully on a playground: if a young kid gets beaten up or even touched, the response is to punch and throw them to the ground. They called this “deterrence,” though it was neither deterrence nor self-defense; it was an aggressive message: “Do not dare defend yourselves, because if you do, we will wipe you off the map.”

Key figures propagating this line during that period included Ariel Sharon, with support from Ben-Gurion himself. Detailed research—such as that by Avi Shlaim—has examined these Israeli strategies in the 1950s. By 1955, Israel had committed horrific massacres in Khan Yunis, including an attack on a hospital. This tactic of attacking hospitals had been employed repeatedly, and its recurrence is simply appalling.

In response, the Egyptians developed a strategy by sending Mustafa Hafez to Gaza to carry out limited operations under Egyptian intelligence. Mustafa Hafez, a well-known figure in Gaza and Palestine, was an Egyptian officer who trained Palestinians from Gaza alongside members of the Egyptian army. Together, they began conducting operations across the border. In turn, the Israelis targeted Mustafa Hafez, eventually assassinating him. He is often cited as one of the first instances where Palestinians were trained and organized after the Nakba—even though these actions were under strict Egyptian oversight.

Nevertheless, these operations had a significant impact: they instilled among Palestinians a belief that they could be trained and armed—if only the Arab states allowed it. At that time, it was clear that the Israeli state would not willingly readmit Palestinians to their homes and lands or grant them political rights out of charity. The prevailing sentiment was that the Israeli state was born of immense violence, which meant that only a military solution was viable. There was no peaceful pathway envisioned that could lead to the return of Palestinian refugees or self-determination on their lands.

Unlike Americans or Europeans—who argued that, having been expelled, Palestinians did not deserve political or national rights—Palestinians maintained that they were indigenous to the land. They rejected the notion that the land should simply be given to newcomers for humanitarian reasons.

Some parties, particularly Pan-Arabists, argued for waiting until the Arab states united and developed the armed capacity needed to liberate Palestine. Others were less convinced. For example, members of the Muslim Brotherhood youth, such as Khalil al-Wazir, were suspicious of Arab regimes. Following the early 1950s conflict between the Muslim Brotherhood and Nasser, they were not enthusiastic about the prospect of the Egyptian government liberating Palestine. Unlike movements that later became Nasserist, those with a Muslim Brotherhood background distrusted Arab regimes tied to imperial powers. Their view was that self-sufficiency was the only viable path forward.

However, there was no clear formula for achieving self-sufficiency. The youth attempted to use the Brotherhood structure for this purpose, but they clashed with the more conservative Brotherhood leadership. This conflict led Khalil al-Wazir and some of his associates to write a well-known letter—complaining about the lack of support for arming the youth and the absence of commitment to armed struggle for Palestine. Ultimately, they left the Brotherhood. Many of those who departed later played key roles in founding Fatah. Others left major parties, such as the Ba’ath, in 1959—particularly during internal splits related to the United Arab Republic.

For instance, the Palestinian/Jordanian branch of the Ba’ath party rejected the shift in policy toward the United Arab Republic. Michel Aflaq initially was enthusiastic about unity with Egypt, but when he realized that Nasser would not provide him with sufficient space in the government, he rebelled against that arrangement.

DENVIR: When they realized that when Nasser declared, “I’m going to be running the show; there will be no political parties,” he was not joking.

TAKRITI: He was not joking. As a result, some Ba’athists left the party—specifically those who opposed Michel Aflaq’s demands to work against the United Arab Republic. This split was led by Abdullah Rimawi, the leader of the Jordanian/Palestinian branch, among others. Rimawi remained with the Ba’athists and sought refuge with Nasser in Cairo, while some younger members began to embrace the emerging idea in the late 1950s that emphasized self-sufficiency and a distinctly Palestinian armed struggle as the foundation of national liberation.

Here, we also recall that Yasser Arafat met with Adil Abd al-Karim in Kuwait, expressing the need for an approach similar to the Algerian revolution. They were inspired not only by that meeting but also by earlier actions—such as the creation of Mustafa Hafez’s units and their operations in occupied territories (what later became known as the state of Israel). They were similarly motivated by the early sabotage operations led by Khalil al-Wazir and the Muslim Brotherhood youth, as well as by an important model that emerged during the Israeli invasion of Gaza and the Sinai in 1956, when a popular front was established in Gaza.

DENVIR: It was the Brotherhood and the Communists.

TAKRITI: Yes, and you had Ba’athists as well, but their experiments were on a smaller scale. The Brotherhood and Communists played significant roles in organizing actions. They had experimented with joint efforts before during an earlier Gaza intifāḍa. So, you had different resistance operations occurring in 1956, which inspired the idea that Palestinians could resist independently of Arab regimes. Notably, there was no Arab regime involved in coordinating that resistance. That’s why I emphasize how crucial Gaza was in the development of the Palestinian struggle—because of this unique situation.

Given this accumulation of experiences, the idea emerged to apply an Algerian model to Palestine. Palestinians looked at Algeria and asked, “Why is it that the Arab states support our Algerian brothers and sisters, allowing them to have their own leadership, restricting their role to arms, diplomacy, and operational support?” But in Palestine, the Arab states intervened in everything. They wouldn’t allow Palestinians to arm or organize themselves.

DENVIR: And they respected the Algerians’ political independence as leaders of their own national liberation struggle.

TAKRITI: Exactly. Meanwhile, in Palestine, the Arab states interfered in every aspect of the struggle. The response from the Arab states was clear: they feared an aggressive settler-colonial state in Israel, and most were tied to imperial powers. They were concerned about their own security and the threat Israel posed to their regimes.

DENVIR: As we already know from Egypt’s administration of Gaza and from what we’ll discover in future episodes about other countries bordering Israel, sheltering Palestinian fedayeen or guerrilla fighters invites Israeli reprisals.

TAKRITI: Yes, that was the Israeli strategy. You can call it the bully strategy or the raging bull strategy. Essentially, Israel’s message was: “If you allow even a single operation or help in any way—even if it’s under the table, and you don’t suppress it—we will come after you.” It’s a very aggressive strategy, and it’s aided by the fact that Israel is heavily armed by Western powers. Some people, who don’t know their history or claim to, argue that the U.S. wasn’t supplying Israel at this time, but that’s not true. The U.S. offered immense support behind the scenes. The British, too, were not neutral—they were quietly helping Israel, including in the nuclear program, providing heavy water, and turning a blind eye to uranium enrichment.

When it comes to the Palestinians, they saw all this and thought, “We need to do something.” Some believed that if the Arab world united, it would liberate Palestine. This was the strategy of the Arab nationalists, and later, it became the strategy of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP). If you look at the PFLP’s logo, it features a map of Palestine and the surrounding Arab region, with an arrow pointing into Palestine. Their view was that Palestine’s liberation could only come through Arab unity.

Fatah, however, had a different approach. They wanted to adopt the Algerian model, the FLN (Front de Libération Nationale). They felt Palestine needed its own national operation front. That’s why they named themselves Ḥarakat at-Taḥrīr al-Waṭanī l-Filasṭīnī (Palestinian National Liberation Movement). Fatah has basically an inversion of the first letters of these words in Arabic. Fatah stands for victory, and it’s a fitting name.

DENVIR: Given the history we’ve already discussed, and the history we’ll continue to explore, what was the significance of Fatah’s formation? What did it signal about the institutionalization of the Palestinian cause as a national liberation movement led by Palestinians?

TAKRITI: Fatah’s formation signified a few key things. First, it made clear that the liberation of Palestine would not come from the Arab states—it had to be carried out by the Palestinians themselves. Second, and this became a core principle of Fatah’s politics, they emphasized that they didn’t want to interfere in the internal politics of Arab states. This became a foundational aspect of Fatah’s approach.

These principles were articulated in two early documents published in 1958 and 1959: Haikal al-bināʾ ath-thawrī (The Edifice of Revolutionary Construction) and Bayān ḥarakatinā (The Manifesto of Our Movement). Both documents emphasize the dispossession and suffering of the Palestinian people, pointing out how Arab states mistreated them during that process. They wanted to communicate that Palestinians could not rely on Arab states for support.

However, and this is important to note, when people describe Fatah as strictly nationalistic and in opposition to Pan-Arabism, I disagree with that interpretation. The prevalent view is that Fatah rejected Arab unity, but my research shows that they weren’t necessarily opposed to incorporating Palestine into a broader Arab project. They just didn’t have faith in the Arab states’ ability to liberate Palestine.

DENVIR: It was more about a strategic assessment. They had seen the results of Arab states claiming leadership of the Palestinian liberation struggle—and it hadn’t worked for several reasons.

TAKRITI: Exactly. It was a lack of faith in the ability of Arab governments to achieve anything meaningful for Palestine. This view became particularly important after the fall of the United Arab Republic (UAR). Initially, there was a lot of enthusiasm when the UAR was formed. People thought that with Egypt and Syria united, liberation was closer than ever. There were even historical comparisons to Saladin uniting Egypt and Syria to liberate Jerusalem from the Crusaders.

For those who would eventually form Fatah, the UAR’s collapse shattered any illusions that Arab unity would liberate Palestine. Even if it had, Fatah believed that the leadership of the Palestinian cause needed to remain in Palestinian hands. Arab states, in their view, could not be trusted to lead the struggle and would act in their own national interests—not in the interests of Palestinians. Even revolutionary governments would act in their own best interests.

DENVIR: It’s important to emphasize once again how significant the Algerian model is, not just within the Arab world—both the Mashriq and Maghreb—but globally, as a symbol of revolutionary decolonization.

TAKRITI: Absolutely. The significance of Algeria manifests in another dimension, aside from the relationship between the Arab states and the Palestinian movement. The question arose: should the Palestinian movement follow the leadership of revolutionary Arab states like Nasser’s Egypt, or should it forge its own independent path?

Fatah answered this by advocating for the latter. They championed the Algerian model, especially in relation to armed struggle. Their belief was that armed struggle was essential to developing the capacity needed to reclaim their land. Fatah’s stance was based on a theory of liberation, specifically focused on how occupied countries can achieve freedom.

In 1965, Fatah issued a well-known pamphlet, The Liberation of Occupied Countries, which they presented at the 1967 Afro-Asian conference in Algeria. The core idea was that armed struggle was the only effective way to challenge colonialism, as it does not respond to diplomacy or negotiations.

This conviction led Fatah to prioritize the development of an armed struggle. In the early years, they focused on building clandestine networks and spreading the idea that Arab states had failed the Palestinian cause. They promoted the necessity of self-dependence and armed resistance through a publication called Filasṭīnūnā, Nidāʾ al-ḥayy (Our Palestine: The Call to Life), produced in Beirut.

Interestingly, Filasṭīnūnā wasn’t just a Palestinian nationalist publication. It was edited by Lebanese figures, including Hani Fakhouri, who I had the pleasure of meeting in Beirut before his passing. Fakhouri later became a bank manager but initially volunteered for the publication. The magazine was registered under the name of Lebanese printmaker Toufiq Houri, who owned the printing shop.

Fatah used this publication to spread their message secretly. They held covert meetings, especially in the Gulf, and established branches in Syria and Jordan, laying the groundwork for a future armed struggle. This period of preparation lasted about six years, from 1959, when Fatah was formalized, to the launch of the armed struggle in 1965.

There are many different stories surrounding these early years, with various factions claiming credit for shaping the movement’s direction. The initial launch of the armed struggle in 1965 is often referred to as the first Inṭilāqa (First Launch), with a second launch occurring after the 1967 defeat.

So, this was a time of major development for Fatah, though much of the work was done in secret.

DENVIR: We’ve talked about Algeria, but this series, by necessity, is focused mostly on the Mashriq. Still, we should keep referencing the Maghreb. Before we move on, can you discuss the role that other Maghreb nations—Libya, Tunisia, Morocco, Western Sahara, and Sudan—played in the Palestinian liberation movement in the 1950s, as well as in broader Arab politics? The Maghreb was a hotbed of radical anti-colonial politics.

TAKRITI: Yes, the Maghreb wasn’t just influenced by other Arab movements; there were direct connections. I mentioned previously that in Cairo, there was a bureau for the Arab Maghreb, which coordinated solidarity efforts across the Arab world. One key figure from the Palestinian side in this effort was actually Yasser Arafat’s brother.

This relationship with the FLN (National Liberation Front) solidified after Algeria’s independence. Palestinian movements were very active in supporting the independence struggles in Morocco, Tunisia, and Algeria. The dynamics were different in Morocco and Tunisia because they weren’t annexed by France in the formal sense, unlike Algeria, which was considered part of France on French maps at the time.

While Morocco and Tunisia’s independence movements were successful, their governments weren’t necessarily revolutionary. For instance, Habib Bourguiba, who led Tunisia’s independence, was a liberal with close ties to the West, and he supported Westernization. His political views soon diverged from those of the Palestinian movement.

On the Moroccan side, the monarchy, closely aligned with the West, was also anti-communist and even assassinated prominent communist figures like Mehdi Ben Barka, with alleged collaboration from Israeli Mossad. These regimes were often categorized by the Arab left as part of what they called the Arab Reaction.

For the Arab left, the main enemies of liberation were the trio of Western imperialism, settler colonial Zionism, and reactionary Arab regimes. They saw these forces as intertwined in a project of regional domination.

DENVIR: Looking ahead, we should note that in 1975, Morocco annexed Western Sahara, establishing it as a colony.

TAKRITI: Yes, Morocco’s annexation of Western Sahara was driven by its own imperial ambitions, especially for the region’s mineral resources. Morocco justified this move under the banner of national unification, claiming it was restoring unity to a region divided by colonialism. However, the people of Western Sahara rejected this narrative. They demanded self-determination and control over their own resources.

Of course, this situation was a direct result of Western colonialism and imperialism, and the issue remains interconnected with broader historical and geopolitical dynamics.

DENVIR: Let’s conclude this episode by discussing Palestinian politics within the newly established state of Israel, which was formed through the mass dispossession and ethnic cleansing of Palestinians. What did Palestinian politics look like throughout the 1950s, a period marked by military rule for Palestinian citizens of Israel? Was the Israeli Communist Party, which was formerly the Palestine Communist Party before the Nakba, the main political force representing Palestinians in Israel during this time?

TAKRITI: After the Nakba, the Palestinians who remained on their lands after the 1948 war faced a disastrous situation. The majority of Palestinian lands were lost, as well as a significant portion of their people.

Sociologically, two things should be noted:

1.        Destruction of Palestinian Cities: All Palestinian cities, except for Nazareth, were destroyed or completely taken over in 1948. As a result, Nazareth was the only Palestinian-majority city within the Israeli state after 1948. Nazareth survived mainly because of its religious significance for Christians. It was small and not considered strategically important, so it wasn’t depopulated like other cities.

2.        Harsh Military Rule: The Palestinian population was placed under harsh military rule and martial law. Every Palestinian community within the boundaries of the Israeli state was under the control of a military official, who decided on every aspect of daily life. This included arbitrary actions, such as forcing individuals to stand by trees until sunset or disrupting classrooms. It was essentially a military occupation, lasting until 1966.

DENVIR: As discussed at the recent World Academic Forum for Palestine, Israel, following in the footsteps of the British mandate, aggressively prevented the emergence of any Palestinian academic or intellectual life. This was aimed at preventing it from happening altogether.

TAKRITI: Yes, Ilan Pappe, who has studied this phenomenon extensively, emphasized this point at the forum. Israel imposed intellectual restrictions, attacking Palestinian thought and education. Unfortunately, this continues to this day. One of the few Palestinian professors in Israeli universities today is Nadera Shalhoub-Kevorkian. She recently endured horrific interrogation, including denial of medical care, harassment, and being placed in inhumane conditions. Her work focused on stopping the genocide in Gaza, and this is exactly why the Israeli authorities target Palestinians who address their issues.

To this day, there are very few Palestinian professors in Israeli universities, and that was certainly the case in the 1950s, when the traditional leaderships of the Palestinian community had been expelled. This left the community to fend for itself without political foundations for survival.

The only organized force capable of operating at the time was the Communist Party, which was allowed to function because it was an Israeli party. Palestinians couldn’t organize themselves around national agendas or Palestinian parties that had existed before. The Communist Party was a viable option because it had a history of unity between its Jewish and Palestinian members. However, this unity had collapsed in 1943 with the establishment of the National Liberation League by Palestinians. By 1943, significant divisions had emerged between the Zionist-leaning Jewish members and the Palestinian members of the Communist Party. These divisions grew during the Great Revolt, and by 1943, the debate between the two groups became more pronounced.

The Palestinian members advocated for a Palestinian state with citizenship rights for all, regardless of background, based on democratic principles. The Jewish members, on the other hand, supported a bi-national state recognizing dual nationality. This divide marked a clear rift between the two wings of the party.

After the Nakba, this debate became irrelevant internally. The establishment of Israel meant that the discussions about the state structure were no longer necessary for the Communist Party, as the Zionist project had been completed. The main debates within the party didn’t resurface until the 1990s when a new Arab party emerged under the leadership of Azmi Bishara. The party, called at-Tajammuʿ (Balad), was a split from the Communist Party, emphasizing Arab identity within the party. The Communist Party had suppressed Arab identity during the 1950s to maintain its ability to organize Palestinians, but at-Tajammuʿ sought to reassert it.

Despite these internal struggles, the Communist Party was the dominant political force during the 1950s and 1960s. It played a crucial role in educating the Palestinian population, as they were heavily discriminated against and excluded from Israeli universities. The Israeli Communist Party provided Palestinians with access to education by facilitating scholarships in the Soviet Union and other Eastern Bloc countries.

The Eastern Bloc played a significant role in supporting Palestinian education, offering scholarships to Palestinian students under Israeli rule. This provided Palestinians with their first access to higher education, and many of them became professionals—doctors, writers, and more. This was especially important for a population that, in 1948, was largely poor and composed of peasants, with a lack of leadership and services.

Despite many critiques of the Communist Party, it provided a cultural space and a platform for resistance. It helped Palestinians build from nothing, offering support in a time of dire need. The party’s influence extended beyond just political activities; it also played a central role in developing a Palestinian intellectual and cultural presence under Israeli rule.

Maha Nassar, a Palestinian scholar at the University of Arizona, captures this experience in her excellent book Brothers Apart. For anyone interested in learning more about the Communist Party’s role during this period, I highly recommend reading her work.

DENVIR: The Communist Party would bring Palestinians to the Soviet Union or other communist countries to receive education.

TAKRITI: Yes, that was a significant part of the Communist Party’s role. It wasn’t just a local phenomenon; it was part of a larger global context in which the Eastern Bloc supported anti-colonial movements around the world. This global support for education and development in colonized regions, including Palestine, is often overlooked in evaluations of the communist experience worldwide.

DENVIR: The “toilers of the East” concept.

TAKRITI: Yes, the “Toilers of the East” was an initial experiment, but later on, every major university in the Eastern Bloc had scholarships for students from across Asia, Africa, and the Middle East, including Palestine. This was crucial for Palestinians under Israeli rule, as it provided them with the education and opportunities they were denied at home.

In 1948, the Palestinian population that remained was mostly poor, composed of peasants, and lacked the leadership and resources that had previously existed. This lack of education and services was a significant challenge. However, the Communist Party played a pivotal role in addressing this issue, offering education and creating a space for Palestinian resistance and cultural development. The party also radicalized itself after the Suez Crisis in 1956 and became more involved in organizing and representing Palestinian interests within Israel.

DENVIR: And at this time throughout the 1950s, is it fair to say that the base of the Israeli Communist Party is overwhelmingly Palestinian while the leadership is somewhat mixed Palestinian and Jewish?

TAKRITI: Yes, you could say that. The Israeli Communist Party was really a space where Palestinians, at least in its base, found some kind of political expression. And as you mentioned, it was also one of the few places where progressive Jewish voices critical of Zionism could emerge. These were individuals who weren’t necessarily full anti-Zionists but were hesitant or reluctant to erase Palestinians from the narrative altogether. So, it wasn’t fully a Jewish or Palestinian space, but it was a unique and complex mixture. On the Jewish side, membership was somewhat limited because of the sociological realities—the settler-colonial nature of the Israeli state meant that for most Jewish people, identifying as working class was complicated. They were part of a colonial system, not part of a natural working-class struggle. A Communist party ideally would represent a proletariat, but in this case, the party had to navigate both class and colonial dynamics, which was a tricky balancing act.

DENVIR: Are there any encounters throughout the 1950s between those Palestinians who remain within the newly established state of Israel and Mizrahi Jews, who, as Ussama Makdisi described on the Dig, represent the second corollary tragedy to the Nakba as a result of Zionism? The very possibility of being an Arab Jew is destroyed throughout the region. Avi Shlaim’s research has shown recently that Zionist agents actually played a major role in terrorizing Iraqi Jews, Baghdadi Jews, so they would make Aliyah to Israel during this period in the 1950s. As Mizrahi Jews’ identity is being remade from that of Arab Jew to Mizrahi Jew, are there moments of encounter and possibility for counterfactual alternative trajectories that might have taken place?

TAKRITI: Yeah, it’s a fascinating question, Daniel. The Mizrahi Jewish experience was incredibly complicated, and as you mentioned, Ella Shohat has been pioneering in bringing attention to this. In the Communist Party, many Mizrahi Jews, especially those from communities like Iraq, had a unique and often painful relationship to the broader Arab world. The Iraqi Jewish community was one of the oldest continuously existing Jewish communities in the world, predating the Jewish community in Palestine. Iraq had a vibrant Jewish community before the establishment of Israel, and their displacement as a result of the Zionist project is a tragedy that has largely been erased from mainstream narratives.

The situation with Iraqis and other Mizrahi Jews really underscores the forced nature of the Zionist project. For example, Avi Shlaim’s research has uncovered how Zionist agents played a role in bombings of synagogues and attacks that were designed to create a sense of insecurity among Iraqi Jews, pushing them to leave Iraq for Israel. This was a process of coercion, not voluntary migration. It’s a very painful chapter, because many of these Jews were displaced from their homes in a manufactured crisis, pushed out by the very people who later claimed to be their saviors. The Zionist leadership didn’t care about the well-being of these communities—they just saw them as numbers, as a necessary part of the population transfer for the newly established state of Israel.

DENVIR: And similarly painful stories in places like Yemen?

TAKRITI: Yes, exactly. The experiences of Jews from Yemen were no different in that sense. The Zionist project in this case was about “central planning,” as they called it. After the expulsion of Palestinians and the creation of Israel, they were looking for more Jews to fill the vacuum. The problem was that most Jews in Europe had already been wiped out by the Holocaust, and the remaining Jews didn’t want to leave their homes, especially those in the U.S. So, Zionist leaders turned their focus to Jews from Arab countries like Iraq, Yemen, Egypt, and Iran—basically, they needed the Jews of the Arab world to help “populate” Israel.

The real tragedy lies in how this process was carried out with racial hierarchies in mind. The Zionist establishment had a very clear racial vision, and at the top of this hierarchy were European Jews, especially those from places like Germany and Russia. Then you had Jews from the Arab world, who were treated very poorly, even though they were often educated and culturally rich. Mizrahi Jews were at the bottom of this hierarchy, and those from Yemen were especially marginalized.

DENVIR: With Yemenis at the bottom or near the bottom?

TAKRITI: Yes, Yemenis were at the very bottom. Iraqis were considered at the top of the Mizrahi scale because they were seen as well-educated and integrated into the urban culture of Baghdad. But even Iraqi Jews, who were supposed to be at the “top” of the Mizrahi ladder, were treated terribly. They were even sprayed with DDT when they arrived in Israel. It was a dehumanizing experience.

This racial hierarchy within the Zionist project is something that Ella Shohat and Avi Shlaim have written about extensively. Avi’s memoir, for instance, gives a deeply personal account of what it was like for an Iraqi Jewish child growing up in Israel, where his Arabness was criminalized and he was treated poorly for speaking Arabic. For many years, he had to repress his identity, but now he’s rediscovering his roots as an Arab Jew. His story is just one of many, but it’s emblematic of the larger experience of Mizrahi Jews in Israel.

DENVIR: In the Communist Party, did the experience of Mizrahi Jews differ from the more mainstream experience?

TAKRITI: Yes, it did. In the Communist Party, Mizrahi Jews, particularly from Iraq, had a very different experience because they had been part of the Arab leftist movements. In fact, Iraqi Jewish communists played a crucial role in the Arab progressive movements before they were forced to leave Iraq. In Israel, many Mizrahi Jews who had been part of these movements found themselves confronting the realities of the new Israeli state, which was violently reshaping identities. But the Communist Party offered them a space where they could continue their activism and confront the contradictions of the Israeli state.

Some of these Mizrahi Jews became prominent writers and activists in the Communist Party, contributing to the party’s publications like Al-Ittihad. Figures like Sami Michael, who was an Iraqi Jew, became notable contributors to the literature of the Communist Party. This aspect of Jewish-Arab collaboration in the Communist Party is an important part of the broader history, but it’s one that has often been overlooked or overshadowed by the mainstream Zionist narrative.

DENVIR: Was there some possibility of the retention of Arab Jewish identity amongst the displaced Eastern Jews that would have allowed for a kind of anti-Zionist solidarity with remaining Palestinians within the state of Israel, or did the very nature of the Zionist project make that impossible?

TAKRITI: It made it very difficult, Daniel. While there were individual cases where Arab Jews felt comfortable interacting with Palestinians, especially within the Israeli state, on a collective level, it was nearly impossible. The situation was tragic for these displaced Jews. They had become refugees themselves, displaced from their homes in the Arab world, and while it wasn’t the fault of Palestinians, they ended up in this dire situation because of a collusion between reactionary Arab states and the Zionist project. This wasn’t a case of Arab Jews being expelled by Palestinians; instead, they were caught in a system where their lives didn’t matter to the Zionists, who were only interested in bolstering the Jewish population in Palestine, regardless of the human cost.

It’s essential to recognize that, unfortunately, Zionism did not care about the welfare of the Arab Jewish populations that it manipulated into its project. This was a movement obsessed with numbers and territorial expansion, and it had no regard for the lives of those uprooted by it. And while Zionist actions might have led to some anti-Semitic incidents in the region, it’s important to remember that most Arab political movements officially rejected anti-Semitism. The tragedy here is multifaceted and painful for all involved.

But on a hopeful note, Daniel, if we ever see a day when the Zionist project is defeated, one of the things that could offer hope would be the reclaiming of Arab Jewish identities. That’s a significant portion of the population, and I think it’s crucial to imagine a future where these identities are restored. I believe that one of the keys to reconciliation in this land could be the recovery of Arab Jewish identities. Arab Jews are culturally and historically closest to Palestinians, and they could play a critical role in bridging the divide. Of course, this is a very optimistic view, and we’re not there yet, but it’s a future worth imagining.

The current reality, though, is that Zionism has created a situation where people are socialized to view one another as enemies, and that’s been reinforced in multiple ways. It’s surreal when you consider that Arab Jews, who are so similar to Palestinians in terms of culture and physical appearance, are put in the position of being conscripts in the Israeli army and are tasked with oppressing those who share so much of their own identity. That’s where contradictions and fissures emerge. It’s a tragic dynamic, but also one that I think could offer pathways to transformation if we could get past this brutal system.

DENVIR: And similarly, how about Ethi opian Jews? How do they fit into the same dynamic?

TAKRITI: It’s very similar for Ethiopian Jews, Daniel. They are racialized by the Zionists in the same way and treated as inferior, but they’re still drafted into the army and placed in positions of power over Palestinians, often in situations where they have to exert control over women, children, the elderly—all people who share their own cultural background in many ways. It’s heartbreaking, especially when you see young Ethiopian Jewish soldiers at checkpoints treating Palestinian elders with humiliation and brutality. Coming from cultures where respect for elders is fundamental, it’s a painful process of negating their own cultural values to fit into this oppressive structure.

This is why I think it’s essential to read Frantz Fanon, to understand colonialism as a degrading process for everyone involved—the oppressed and the oppressors. The settlers and the natives alike are degraded by this system. And we see that degradation in real time in Palestine today, where these young conscripts are forced to take on roles that degrade their own humanity while simultaneously oppressing others.

The entire situation is deeply tragic, and it’s a stark reminder of the ways in which colonialism distorts human relationships and creates systems of violence that hurt all involved, whether they’re the oppressed or the oppressors. That’s the heartbreaking reality.

DENVIR: It’s truly painful to witness. How do you see this evolving, especially considering the ongoing genocide? What might liberation look like in a post-genocide reality?

TAKRITI: Honestly, Daniel, it’s hard to say what liberation will look like after genocide. The reality on the ground is incredibly painful right now. The situation is so dire, and there’s no clear path forward. But I think one thing that we have to consider, and something Palestinians in the 1950s predicted, is that Zionism’s ultimate goal is total elimination. When Palestinians were forced into exile or put under military rule by the Israeli state, they were trying to resist this fate. They knew that if they didn’t act then, their future would become even more dire. They foresaw a situation where the Zionist state would become so powerful that liberation would be almost impossible, especially after acquiring nuclear weapons. They feared that the process of liberation would eventually become irreversible, and the opportunity to return to their land would slip away.

For Palestinians, liberation wasn’t just about freeing the land—it was about returning to it. It’s vital to understand that Palestinian politics, particularly in the 1950s, were rooted in refugee politics. It was about creating conditions where Palestinians could go back to their homes, to their villages, to their cities. This return is central to their vision of liberation.

Unfortunately, in the West, many people misunderstand this. The idea of Palestinians returning to their land is often portrayed as the eradication of Israel, but that’s a misreading of the situation. Palestinians don’t seek to eradicate Israel—they seek to return to their homes. It’s an incredibly anti-refugee position to misinterpret their demands for return as some kind of “eradicationist” plan.

Ultimately, though, the question of what liberation will look like after genocide is still open. What we know for sure is that the current situation is unsustainable. The key, I believe, lies in reclaiming Palestinian identities, Arab Jewish identities, and ultimately, finding a way to bridge the divide between the settlers and the native populations. But how we get there, after all the pain and destruction, is a question for the future. It’s not something we can easily answer today. But it’s worth imagining, especially in the context of liberation.
