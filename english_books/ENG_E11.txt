Thawra Ep. 11 – Ba’ath Seize Power



This Episode Overview:

The ecstatic utopian possibilities of 1958 in Iraq, Syria, and Egypt started to fall apart fast. In Iraq, Abd al-Karim Qasim had led a Free Officers coup that overthrew the imperialist-aligned Hashemite monarchy and brought the Iraqi Communist Party to the heights of power. But, as we discussed in the last episode, Qasim—after suppressing the Ba’athists and the Nasserists—started to clip the communists’ wings.

In 1961, things in Iraq got even more complicated, as Kurds led by Mulla Mustafa Barzani, a one-time Qasim ally, launched the Aylul Revolution against the central state. This was the most serious quest for Kurdish self-determination since the Republic of Mahabad in 1940s Iran. Meanwhile, this Iraqi Kurdish rebellion was openly backed by Iran. More secretly and controversially, Barzani received support from Israel—behind the backs and against the wishes of many of his supporters.

In February 1963, all these conflicts and contradictions in Iraq came to a head. Ba’athists, Nasserists, and other nationalist military officers overthrew Qasim in a coup, after which the Ba’athists unleashed a wave of bloody terror against Iraqi communists, the region’s most important reservoir of grassroots left-wing power.

The other giant event of 1958 was Syria uniting with Egypt in the United Arab Republic (UAR), a superstate ruled by Nasser that, for so many, seemed to be a giant first step toward fulfilling the pan-Arab dream of a unified Arab nation strong enough to defeat Western imperialism and Zionism.

In 1961, the UAR was effectively abolished after officers in Syria staged a coup that led to Syria’s secession. While Nasser continued to maintain strong support in Syria—then called the UAR’s northern region—he had also alienated many Syrian military officers, nearly all political parties, and the bourgeoisie. Nasser had weakened officers’ political power and relocated them far from home to Egypt. Political parties were banned, and Nasser appointed Syrian officer Abdul Hamid al-Sarraj to oversee repression of dissent. The economic elites in Syria were furious at Nasser’s nationalizations and moves to break monopolies.

Syria’s secession and the effective end of the UAR was a cataclysmic event for Syria, Egypt, and the entire Arab world. The secessionist governments that followed the coup ruled on behalf of the Syrian bourgeoisie. Syria reoriented toward the West and moved to reverse Nasser’s nationalizations and other policies deemed hostile to capital. Secession was, unsurprisingly, a huge blow to Nasser and the Arab nationalist project he had led.

In response, Nasser shifted Egypt to the left, embracing the concept of Arab socialism. But things were not settled in Syria. The Syrian secessionist government was overthrown by a Ba’athist military coup in March 1963. A reminder here for listeners: all these events were happening in multiple countries simultaneously. The 1963 Ba’ath coup in Syria occurred less than a month after the February 1963 Iraqi coup that overthrew Qasim. Indeed, the Iraqi coup inspired Ba’athist officers to act in Syria.

But even as Ba’athists seized power in Syria and Iraq, their party was fragmenting—between different nation-states, between ideologies, and between military and civilian leadership. For example, the heavily Palestinian Jordanian branch of the Ba’ath Party rebelled against the party leadership’s growing hostility toward Nasser. Most importantly, in 1959, Syrian Ba’athist officers whom Nasser had stationed in Egypt formed a Syrian Ba’ath Military Committee. It was a rival base of power within the party, led by figures like Salah Jadid and Hafez al-Assad.

This power base, in a 1966 coup, sidelined the party’s traditional civilian leadership figures—like Michel Aflaq and Salah al-Din al-Bitar. The dominance of Syrian Ba’ath military officers would play out in a sequence of events that ended with Hafez al-Assad seizing total power in 1970.

The coups continued in Iraq as well. Just nine months after the Ba’ath had helped lead the coup overthrowing Qasim, Nasserists and other nationalists moved against the Ba’ath in the November 1963 coup, firmly establishing Abdul Salam Arif—Qasim’s one-time ally and co-conspirator—as leader. But the Ba’ath would be back with a more decisive coup down the road in 1968—a coup that would bring Ahmed Hassan al-Bakr, alongside his deputy Saddam Hussein, to power.

An event that, along with Assad’s 1970 coup in Syria, we’ll explore in much more detail as we enter this series’ final episodes.

Why dwell for so long on this series of cascading defeats for left-wing and progressive projects in the region? The rival radical projects of pan-Arabism and communism suffered huge blows in the early 1960s. So did Nasser and Qasim, the era’s most significant Arab anti-imperialist leaders.

Meanwhile, the Ba’ath—once an ideological and idealistic party—became increasingly dominated by military men who turned it into an instrument for raw domination. In large part, these defeats signaled changing political coordinates in the Middle East: the beginning of the end of a certain kind of left-wing project and the rise of a new kind of military regime—one that raised slogans of social liberation and anti-imperialism while prioritizing power consolidation.

Initially, “Ba’ath” political figures give cover. “Salah al-Din Bitar” initially gives cover to the coup carried out by “Karim Nahawi” and the Damascene officers against the unity. This causes issues. So “Muhammad Umran”, “Salah Jadid,” and “Hafez al-Assad,” these characters become central. They are actually sitting around and planning what to do next in relation to the “Qodsi” government.

The big factor that galvanizes them into action is actually what happens in “Iraq” just a couple of weeks earlier. At the end of February, we have the “Ba’athist” state control essentially in “Iraq” through a limited army action. It didn’t take much orchestration; it wasn’t even a very elaborate army action. The air force went in, bombed an airfield, and a couple of units got mobilized.

They marched on the Capital, took over the sensitive main areas, including the radio station, made false proclamations about the fall of the regime, and then besieged the president in the Ministry of Defense. They fought hard for the Ministry of Defense, managed to take it over, and got the premier to surrender. Eventually, we had a situation where the game was executed, and this regime was over. The people in the military committee of the Baath Party thought, “We could do that.” Some had already tried to do it and failed, like the previous year.

It’s not that they didn’t have longstanding issues with the existing government. The March Eighth crew in Syria cannot be detached from its broader context, especially from its Iraqi context.

D: Is the civilian Baath leadership involved in the Syrian 1963 Baath coup, or is it entirely the military committee?

T: The political leadership, and this becomes a matter of contention, according to Munif Razzaz, were not told about the coup. They did not authorize it; it came as a surprise. But then he says, “Look, we give it our blessing,” because of what he considers to be miscalculations on the part of the leadership.

Munif Razzaz considered, and a lot of the old Baathists later on in their self-critique considered that they made a mistake. The national leadership made a mistake because it thought it could approve of this coup and then form a Baathist government, which is what happened, but without establishing clear guidelines for what the relationship was going to look like between the civilian wing of the party in government and the military side of the party that carried out the coup.

He says there should have been a very clear understanding of the relationship between the two. In his view, the military committee was acting independently of the party. These were people with Baath backgrounds who were speaking in the name of the Baath, but they were not the Baath. They were not consulting the Baath Party when they were making decisions.

He said that what should have happened was a regulation of the relationship. A lot of Baathists said later on, in hindsight, that from the very beginning, they should have said, “We’re not going to participate in government if we don’t have full control over the government.” One option could have been that the military committee would ensure Baath ascendancy in the military while the civilians took care of domestic affairs.

Another option could have been to have two parties, affiliated or two wings. Organizationally, there could have been many formulas, but to the outside world, everyone was speaking about Baathist rule in Syria. The funny thing about what was happening was that this was Baathist rule without Baathists because the real Baath’s official leadership consisted of traditional leaders, and many of its civilian cadres were not the ones really in power. It was an inner circle of Baathist military officers.

D: As we’ll talk about near the end of this episode, the issue of the increasing autonomy of the Baath military committee and Baathist military officers from the Baath Party’s civilian leadership came to a head in a really nasty way in 1966 and again in 1970.

After the 1963 coup that overthrew Qasim in Iraq, it was Qasim’s one-time coup accomplice and later rival and enemy, Arif, who took his place as the leader of a regime made up of a rather diverse and ultimately very conflictual anti-Qasim coalition. Less than a year after Arif and the Baath cooperated in the coup to overthrow Qasim, Arif and his allies purged Baathists in yet another coup.

I am going to attempt to summarize this brief period by separating the February and November Iraqi coups of 1963. There were pushes to unify Syria, Egypt, and Iraq, but they all fell apart quickly. There was growing conflict between various factions of the Baath, including factions within and between the Syrian and Iraqi Baath, such as an Iraqi so-called *left Baath faction that, with the support of the Syrian Baath military committee, purged the Iraqi party of Michel Aflaq’s followers. There was also conflict between a Baath-controlled militia called the National Guard and top Iraqi military officers.

There were conflicts between Baathists and Nasserists and members of the movement of Arab nationalists. Additionally, there was pressure from the Soviet Union around the Baath slaughter of Iraqi communists.

How did all these conflicts and contradictions that pervaded the regime that came to power in February 1963, and other conflicts and contradictions that I failed to mention, lead to the November 1963 coup against the Baath?

T: First of all, Abd al-Salam Arif was not a Baathist, and this is important to note. He was in coalition with the Baath or had allied with the Baath in his quest to overthrow Qasim, but Abd al-Salam Arif was closer to a Nasserist conception of things. He was very taken by the image of Gamal Abdel Nasser and his policies. However, he was willing to work with the Baath, and initially, during his coup, the Baath played a very important role in bringing him to power. This meant that in the initial period, the Baath was the dominant force in Iraq after the overthrow of Qasim.

However, the Baath leadership in Iraq was confronted with a challenge: their power in the army was limited. They had officers in the army, but they were spread thin. We're talking here about local leaderships that were really running the show, not the Ba’ath national command which operated on the pan Arab level. We're talking here about people like Ali Saleh al-Sa’adi, who was really pushing for a development of a strong alternative power base for the Ba’ath. The solution to this was to establish the National Guard, which became essentially a parallel structure to the army. That force, which numbered in the thousands, was not particularly disciplined. Some of its practices had to do with taking revenge against the communists who had previously massacred many Baathists, killing and torturing them in earlier years.

Now they felt they needed to do the same to their foes. Instead of thinking about the political future of the country, they were engaging in a purge of the communists and essentially the politics of vendetta. Some members had nothing to do with the Baath altogether; some were just local thuggish types looking for personal gain. They were not particularly ideologically disciplined, which caused resentments within the army and also led Abd al-Salam Arif to fear potential Ba’ath action against him.

Eventually, structurally, this was an unsustainable position bound to lead to a serious contradiction. Abd al-Salam Arif did what he needed to do by essentially removing the Baath from power before they could move against him.

Was it inevitable? Could the Baath have taken other steps? As we have mentioned in previous episodes, there were always alternatives. It is easy to judge these things in hindsight, but yes, I believe it could have been done differently. The Baath leadership at the time was beginning to feel that it was losing control over its own party. I’m talking here about the national leadership that was experiencing a mercurial tendency affecting the party rather than a steady, long-term vision based on the principles of the party.

And this came as a result of the increasing entry of the militarizing trend into the party. We’ll talk about this more when it comes to “Syria” as well.

D: And “Michel Aflaq” disapproved of the “Ba’ath” reign of terror against communists in the wake of the coup.

T: Yes, there was the national leadership in general in the “Ba’ath” party, although they were vehemently opposed and talked very bitterly about communist actions against them earlier, including different massacres and various attacks, especially the “Mosul” episode, which played a big role in shaping those resentments. Additionally, the hunting down of “Ba’athists” and “Baghad” elsewhere during the ascendency of the “Communist Party” under “Qasim” contributed to this tension. However, at the same time, they believed that this was too extreme and that it should not have been as bloody as it was. And they were right.

D: After “Abdul Salam Arif” died in a 1966 plane crash, his brother “Abdul Rahman Arif” took power. What sort of regime under both the Arif brothers did this second coup of November 1963 put in place? This regime lasted until the next “Ba’ath” coup in 1968.

Union with “Nasser’s Egypt” was once again on the agenda as the government nationalized major industries. As Egypt’s sole political party, the “Arab Socialist Union,” merged with a similar party set up in “Iraq,” union did not happen. Indeed, “Nasserists” would increasingly turn against the Iraqi regime.

“Abed,” what should listeners understand about this regime that dominated “Iraq” under both Arif brothers in the years leading up to the 1968 “Ba’ath” coup, which would consolidate long-term “Ba’ath” hegemony?

T: Let’s start first with the “Nasserist” bit. As I mentioned, initially under “Abdul Salam Arif,” and both under him and “Abdul Rahman,” they felt close to the “Nasserist” ideal. In the case of “Abdul Salam,” he tried early on to achieve unity with Egypt, which became the basis of his coalition after he overthrew the “Ba’athists.” The remaining partners he had from the party world were nationalists who were very committed to the idea of establishing unity with Egypt, seeing themselves as essentially “Nasser’s” voluntary arm in the region. This was the basis of his coalition with them, and he did want to undertake unity with Egypt.

But there were problems. The main and biggest issue was that “Nasser” was not interested. He was worried about repeating the mistakes that had happened during the time of unity with “Syria.” He was concerned that “Iraq” was far from “Egypt,” even further away from “Syria.” Logistically, it would have been very difficult to send troops there or conduct various operations; he would have had to rely on air transport, unlike “Syria,” which has a Mediterranean shore and is close to “Egypt.” There were all sorts of logistical hurdles, and the countries bordering “Iraq” and “Egypt,” separating them from each other, were hostile to “Nasser” and this government.

So, there were difficulties with this, and I believe that “Nasser” was fairly confident that the internal situation in “Iraq” would bring him a lot of headaches. This meant that, although there was a political will on the part of “Arab” leaders to conduct unity negotiations in very good faith, on “Nasser’s” side, there was less enthusiasm. Once you removed this unity agenda, which was the main goal in many ways, the internal contradictions began to emerge.

Of course, “Abdul Rahman Arif” had his own problems with “Arab nationalists.” Eventually, he fell out with them as well, leading to the continuation of a new base of power that relied on kinship networks, ensuring that the state and the army were well-staffed with these networks. This became a new power base that “Arif” relied on. He came from a powerful tribe, the “Jumaila,” and had “Jumaila” members in different positions, especially in the army. This became part of the way of conducting rule in the country.

D: The “Iraqi Communist Party” had, as we’ve discussed, suffered absolutely brutal, devastating, shattering repression under the “Ba’ath” after that first 1963 coup. Under the Arif brothers, “Hana Batatu” recounts this real roller coaster of an experience for the communists in terms of their orientation toward the Arifs and also towards “Nasser.” This involved major differences between the Communist leadership committee abroad and the leadership committee on the ground in “Iraq.” By 1967, there was even a formal split, with an ultra-left, Maoist-inspired “Iraqi Communist Party” central command calling for armed guerrilla struggle.

How would you summarize the Iraqi communists’ various positions and politics, as well as their actual position in Iraqi politics after 1963?

T: Well, I mean, you have here a party that was trying to recover from a very serious hit early on during the initial onset of the regime of “Abdul Salam Arif.” Of course, there was the hit that the communists took at the hands of the “Ba’ath.” But then there was a period of greater openness towards them after “Arif” got rid of the “Ba’athists.”

So, this counter-cord that he launched against the “Ba’ath,” or whatever we want to call it, the removal of the “Ba’ath” from positions of power and influence meant that there was an opportunity to relax the atmosphere. This led to a series of debates and different tendencies emerging within a party like the Communist Party. Because they had taken significant hits, many of the party members who could be called realists felt it was better to keep some channels with the governing authorities rather than carry out any major insurgency against them or any sort of armed struggle. The feeling was that they essentially didn’t have the base to do that.

In contrast, the more ultra factions you refer to, especially those influenced by Maoist ideas of guerrilla warfare, particularly relying on the countryside and the peasantry, saw some form of horizon for change through adopting new strategies and tactics. These developments also cannot be viewed in isolation from the “Soviet” split dynamics, which affected all communist parties. In the 1960s, every communist party had to experience this, including the “Communist Party of Iraq.”

There was no doubt that you had people in every communist party in the world who saw great hope and inspiration in “China,” while others remained loyal to the “Soviet Union.” In the “Arab” region, the Soviet direction was the strongest within communist parties for many reasons. However, this does not mean that there was no development and growth of Maoist ideas.

You also have a third issue at play, which is, of course, much of the internal dynamics are dominated by the war in “Kurdistan,” which did not start during the reign of the “Arif” brothers, but it started earlier. By now, it had developed significantly, especially under “Abdul Rahman Arif,” who took power after the airplane crash suffered by “Abdul Salam Arif.” By the way, it was initially reported as a helicopter crash, but most theories now say it’s an airplane crash.

In any case, the reign of “Abdul Rahman Arif” starts with a major offensive against the forces of “Mulla Barzani.” He really makes a big mobilization, but it’s difficult to reign in.

It’s also a terrain where Kurdish forces are fighting on their own territory, which they know very well. Of course, they were supported at the time by both the Shah and the Israeli state. Sometimes, Israelis aggrandize their role, claiming they made the plans for this, and they often portray the defeat as part of their global propaganda defense. But they definitely played a role in this for sure.

This situation illustrated the possibilities of defeating an organized army in difficult terrain in the countryside. All of these factors meant there was a lot of soul-searching going on during this period in the communist ranks. “What is to be done?” was a big question for them.

Another difficulty they faced was regarding the government of Arif and the government of Nasser, which inspired their style of governance. Many of the economic policies and the general facade of government adopted by the Arif Brothers were inspired by Egyptian forms of Arab socialism. Some of these policies were not necessarily seen as bad by the communists; they were not opposed to land reform or its expansion, nor to various policies undertaken by Nasser and the Arif Brothers.

However, some policies caused problems for the economy, leading to a clash with the bourgeoisie, which was not easy to manage. This led to issues like capital flight, particularly due to the nationalization of banks and insurance companies. It was a complicated period to be a communist; these were not governments that fully implemented a program they agreed with. Yet, at the same time, they had partial aspects of what the communists wanted to see in the world.

At the end of the day, these were governments that were far more inclined toward an Arab nationalist agenda, or a pan-Arabist agenda, rather than a classical communist party agenda.

D: Let’s finish this episode, which will end on the eve of the 1967 War, in Syria. In February 1966, the Syrian Ba’ath military committee and allied forces, often described as left-wing and neo-Ba’athist, staged a coup against the party’s old guard—familiar figures from this series that listeners have heard a lot about, such as Michel Aflaq and Salah al-Din al-Bitar.

How did the 1966 coup come about, and what should we make of this divide within the Syrian Ba’ath party at this point? This military committee purportedly supported the party’s left wing, while the civilian old guard was described as being on the right. Additionally, how did the coup impact Ba’ath power dynamics on a regional level, specifically between the Iraqi and Syrian branches?

T: To understand this episode, we must go back to the United Arab Republic. When the Ba’ath party agreed with Nasser’s directive to dissolve the party, and when Michel Aflaq did dissolve it, there was discontent within parts of the party. This led to a situation in which any reconstitution of the party in the future had to take place in an atmosphere where a gap had now existed.

When you dissolve a party, you cannot guarantee that the new party that will reemerge later will be the same. This was part of the difficulty faced by the old leadership of the Ba’ath. The old Ba’ath had three prominent figures: Salah al-Din al-Bitar, Akram Horani, and Michel Aflaq. However, in the new atmosphere, different leaderships emerged without knowledge of these founding figures or other important ones, such as Munif Razaz, who played a significant role and was removed by the school because he was the head of the national committee at that time (the pan-Arab one).

People like that had no idea that there were others organizing in the name of the Ba’ath within the Syrian army, who were not part of its traditional party structure or leadership and did not take directives from that leadership. In particular, we are talking about members of the Syrian Air Force who were in Cairo during the events mentioned earlier in this episode.

The key character that played a significant role in galvanizing these officers was Salah Jadid. He was a key player after the formation of the so-called military committee and had a bumpy relationship with the national leadership of the Ba’ath as well as with some sectors of the party in the army that he initially worked with to overthrow the Qudsi government and institute the revolution of 1963.

The military committee was established, and its members had good relations, having served together. In the case of at least two key members, they came from the same region and sect, the Alawite sect, and had worked together, including Hafiz al-Assad and Salah Jadid.

At the same time, they needed a figurehead to carry out a significant move like the 1963 coup. They found that figure in a dynamic and well-regarded officer, Amin al-Hafez, who was at that time in semi-exile working as a military attaché in the Syrian embassy in Buenos Aires. They brought him back, and he became the president of the new Republic after the coup.

Initially, it seemed like they were all working together and everything was fine, but differences soon surfaced between them. These differences were complicated and not easy to categorize. Amin al-Hafez developed a power base that relied more on public outreach. He presented himself as a popular leader, in touch with the people, focusing on his public image.

Meanwhile, Salah Jadid worked quietly within the military, building his power base. This marked a significant shift in the military internally at this time. He made use of the purges that had occurred against the Damascus officers earlier and against other officers seen as part of the regime of secession, which was considered a crime.

While some officers were purged, there were replacements and new recruitments that were concentrated on people from his region and sect, resulting in a predominantly large percentage of Alawites in the Syrian army. This situation is sensitive, as it involves sectarian dynamics, but it’s important to acknowledge this reality.

In addition to building a base through admissions to military academies and similar means, Salah Jadid also worked strategically to isolate Amin al-Hafez in smart ways.

Amin al-Hafez was essentially outplayed by him every step of the way. Sometimes, he nominated people who were close to Amin al-Hafez for important positions to create splits in the ranks of the coalition around Amin al-Hafez.

He was able to attract people who had previously been attacking him and were strongly in the Hafez camp, as well as people who did not belong to his sect. He built all sorts of alliances with people like Mustafa Tlass, for example, who was a Sunni officer that used to attack Salah Jadid and he eventually joined him.

Amin al-Hafez also committed a series of mistakes. Inside the military committee, he waged a campaign accusing Salah Jadid and Hafez al-Asad and the rest of that crew of being sectarian and of executing a sectarian takeover of the army.

In a secular party like the “Ba’ath,” which is based on the idea of Arab nationalism—not just Syrianism—sectarian discourse is seen as vulgar. Although this rhetoric could generate some sympathy in certain circles, it ultimately left a sour taste. The “Ba’ath” party had always had a significant number of minorities within it.

We have discussed before that radical parties of this sort did not appeal to the petty bourgeoisie in the cities. Traditionally, the largest populations were in the two main trading cities—“Aleppo” and “Damascus”—but also in places like “Homs,” which are Sunni majority cities. The countryside, however, is very diverse, with a largely agrarian “Druze” population in the south, specifically in the “Huran” region. Alongside them, of course, there are many others, and a significant part of the countryside remains diverse.

Then you have the “Alawi” countryside along the Mediterranean coast, where again, all these areas are mixed. I hate to talk about them in sectarian terms, and I believe it is very problematic. If we were to have a future revival of a progressive project, we need to find ways to discuss sectarian dynamics without falling into the traps of just talking about them in that way.

Nevertheless, the reality is that the “Ba’ath” party recruits from these areas, and the message appeals to them. People who come from agrarian and underprivileged backgrounds, for instance, are likely to resonate with this message. Of course, there was also a traditional base of the party composed of teachers and similar professionals, but another base sociologically depended on those social classes.

That’s why many “Alawis,” as well as “Druze,” “Christians,” and others, entered the party. In any case, “Amin al-Hafez” launching accusations of sectarianism did not go over well. “Salah Jadid” was very careful to make use of these mistakes.

Another problem was the contradiction within the military committee between “Salah Jadid” and “Amin al Hafez,” as “Hafez al-Asad” was building his power behind the scenes throughout this process. Later, he would get rid of “Salah Jadid” himself, as we will see later in this series. However, during this period, the main conflict on the surface was between “Salah Jadid” and “Amin al-Hafez.”

There was also another conflict involving the national committee, specifically the “Pan Arab Committee” and the “Syrian Qotri Committee,” which basically refers to the Syrian committee. The “Baath” party did not believe in borders between Arab states, referring to the nation as the Arab nation. The national committee was responsible for all Arab countries.

Additionally, some conflicts arose from the founding fathers wanting to retain control, even though they were living in a very different age, with new generations emerging. As we know from previous episodes, the most famous figure and the most important founder is “Michel Afflaq.” His close friend, “Salah al-Din Bitar,” came in second. “Michel Afflaq” was the secretary general of the party and was essentially at the head of the national committee, but he decided to leave the leadership of that committee around that time. That’s when they brought in “Munif Razaz” as the new secretary general.

While “Afflaq” wanted to maintain a role from behind the scenes, a problem emerged. Both he and “Salah al-Din Bitar” wanted to influence matters without being officially on the national committee. Meanwhile, the leadership of the national committee, recommended by them, was new, including “Munif Razaz,” who was formerly the head of the local Jordanian committee and had been a political prisoner in Jordan. A highly respected intellectual and author, he came in under the impression that he and “Afflaq” shared similar views on what should be done next.

His plan involved initiating good relations with the military officers, as there was no way to eliminate them entirely, while simultaneously creating conditions where they could not control civilian government and would essentially be pushed back into their barracks. To achieve this, “Munif Razaz” aimed to avoid escalating tensions with the military, believing that an escalation would lead them to retaliate and destroy him. They had the guns, and he did not, as simple as that.

However, “Afflaq” and “Bitar” had slightly different positions. “Afflaq” wanted to expose the takeover of the party by military figures and was very bitter about it. He wished to publish statements addressing the reasons behind the resignation of these characters who took the party in different directions. There had been a major decline in party education under their influence, leading to many problems.

He essentially sought to criminalize and expose them. On the other hand, “Salah al-Din Bitar,” his lifelong associate and close friend, wanted to take it to the next level by directly clashing with them, not just publishing critiques. He wants to remove them and he aimed for the national committee to serve as an instrument for this purpose, creating tension between the national committee under “Razaz” and “Afflaq” and “Bitar”.

Thus, various power bases were developing at this time, all within the “Ba’ath” party but representing different generations. There were the founding fathers, the early generation represented by “Munif Razaz,” and newcomers from military backgrounds, who were viewed as hijacking the party for agendas unrelated to its original ideology.

If you want to complicate it further, the “Salah Jadid” faction, in its war with both the “Amin al-Hafez” regime and the traditional party leadership and the national committee, essentially uses left-wing ideology as its banner. They accuse these people of being essentially right-wing.

D: Do the so-called “neoba’athists.” have a substantial ideological difference with the traditional Ba’athists that they overthrew, or is that more of a pretext to legitimate their power play?

T: Yes, there is a significant element of using leftist slogans for internal power grabbing; there’s no doubt about it. However, under “Salah Jadid” and the group he brings into power, he does incorporate some leftist figures within the Ba’ath Party and implements a project that entails some leftist economic policies. Additionally, they commit to a geopolitical policy that raises a more left-wing stance, particularly concerning “Palestine” and, to some extent, “Yemen.”

They try to position themselves as to the left of “Nasser” and end up clashing with him, aiming to demonstrate greater commitment to anti-colonial armed revolts than he had. This, as we shall see in subsequent episodes, opens a pathway for the Palestinian revolution, as this is when Syria allows for training and facilitates the emergence of a Palestinian guerrilla warfare movement, especially under the direction of “Fatah.”

This dynamic creates serious problems for Nasser, who feels that the Syrian government is launching a verbal assault on him, accusing him of cowardice for not confronting “Israel” and not confronting Arab reactionary regimes. He seeks a way out of the “Yemen” crisis and the clash with the Saudis, attempting to avoid a direct clash with the Israelis at a time when he is not ready for it, and the Arab world is not prepared either. Meanwhile, the Syrian government is adamantly pushing the line that a liberation war is necessary now, questioning why they aren’t supporting Palestinian liberation immediately.

Is that a traditional left position? It’s hard to define; on the surface, it looks like a leftist stance, but it also has elements that might be described as ultra- or adventurist, particularly regarding geopolitics. Were some people sincere about it? Yes, probably, but not necessarily Salah Jadid though. Figures like “Dr. Ibrahim Makhous”, “Dr. Nureddin (Nordel) al-Atasi" and “Yusuf al-Zaim”  who served with the National Liberation Front in “Algeria,” are associated with this movement. They had volunteered there as medical doctors, developing sympathy for the idea of a Palestinian liberation capacity along the Algerian model. They were committed to Third Worldism and various liberation causes.

In short, the answer to your question is both yes and no.

D: To close, this episode chronicles the failure of the “United Arab Republic” in Syria and Egypt and the failure of “Qasim” and the “Communist Party” to achieve a stable, hegemonic revolutionary regime in “Iraq.” An increasingly splintered Ba’ath Party played a critical role in both countries.

What should we make of this arc from 1958 through the early 1960s, characterized by the rise and fall of these utopian revolutionary dreams that defined this era of Arab political radicalism? Alternatively, how do you assess the diverse Arab radicalisms that surged forth first during the mandate era and then intensified after the “Nakba”? Where does this leave all these competing radical projects on the eve of the 1967 War?

T: The first difficulty faced by all these projects is that the question of the state was not resolved in the region. On the nationalist end, all these parties emerged from a desire to unify at least the former Ottoman territories that had an Arab majority. Many were later more ambitious, seeking to unify the Arab world from the Gulf to the ocean. Their visions seemed realistic for a short while, particularly with the establishment of the United Arab Republic in the 1950s—a period marked by burgeoning hopes. These were countries that had just gained independence and were embarking on their journeys, filled with the optimism of potential unification.

At the same time, more radical parties aimed for social justice policies and greater redistribution. Some desired radical socialist policies, while others aimed for social democracy. Regardless, the left dominated the political arena.

D: They all wanted to see the defeat of imperialism, Zionism, and Arab reaction.

T: Yes, it is quite significant; if we look at the overall picture, it was not just a dark picture from leftist perspective, despite setbacks ,in many ways it was a golden age for the Arab left. The overthrow of monarchies in Egypt and the removal  of Egypt from the Western camp marked ambitious redistributive policies: free education, expanded healthcare, and the construction of schools directly benefited people’s lives, making even Nasser’s competitors acknowledge the positive aspects of his governance.

We also saw an expansion of the women’s movement, which occurred in Iraq as well. Iraq exited the Western camp, entered into non-alignment and achieved nationalization projects, making significant strides toward controlling its national resources, especially oil. They supported revolutionary movements in other parts of the Arab world, just as Egypt did. Syria did not enter the Western orbit due to internal struggles; it also did not join the Baghdad Pact, making it one of the three most important Arab countries in the Mashreq.

On the surface, there was still a unified front regarding the Palestinian question, even if that did not translate into military support for Palestinians at that stage. Leaders like Nasser publicly insisted on the right of return for Palestinian refugees and their national political rights, no resettlement but repatriation, reflecting mobilizations by these refugees themselves.

So, on multiple levels, radical movements on the left made major strides, which is important to remember. However, none of these countries were rich or powerful enough to change the overall game played in the region. A powerful Western imperial presence remained entrenched. The West relied on countries like “Turkey,” which entered NATO and became crucial during the Cold War, as well as “Iran” under the Shah, suppressing the aspirations of the Iranian people by overthrowing the democratically elected “Mossadegh” government. The West also controlled the countries with the most vital resources, particularly oil.

And the regimes they oversee have the benefit of a certain geopolitical stability that can be afforded by having a great power, a superpower backer. You know, if you’re fully backed by the United States, and earlier backed by Britain, you already have a stabilizing presence as a force. You have someone who could fund you if you fall, arm you if needed, and provide you with diplomatic cover. That’s an important part of the story of the stability of Arab governments in the conservative states that were allied with the West.

Often, the story of the Arab left is told through their prisons. They’ll say, “Look, unlike these people who are constantly fighting with each other, we remained stable, and we offered our people stability and wealth.” However, in reality, that stability was not internally generated; it came at the cost of losing a substantial element of national sovereignty. This dynamic played out in a significant way. The other price of this stability was extreme inequality, as well as a lack of interest in any form of social justice or political justice, particularly on the anti-colonial level, for instance, in the case of Palestine. It was not the Saudis that facilitated the Algerian revolution; rather, it was Nasser who played a major role. While they helped a little, their involvement was minor compared to Nasser’s impact.

So, when we make a general assessment of what the left was confronting and what it achieved in that period, these are some of the issues we must consider. Of course, there are many other factors, but we would need an entire podcast just for that.
